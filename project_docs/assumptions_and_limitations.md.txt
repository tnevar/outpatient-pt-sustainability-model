CMS 2023 Assumptions and Limitations

* The project’s primary objective is to model state-level outpatient PT sustainability under workforce and Medicare-based economic assumptions, with a secondary analysis of therapist value capture using PT labor share of modeled revenue.
* Utilizing public available CMS Medicare B data, average reimbursement per unit sourced for each state. This number was utilized to establish revenue amount per unit for each state that was utilized across UPH scenarios and throughout the project. This, avg\_payment\_per\_service is treated as per-unit reimbursement
* Two-Lens Interpretation (Nominal vs RPP-Adjusted)
* This project uses two complementary interpretation lenses rather than one universal metric set.
* Nominal lens (primary for sustainability):
* The base sustainability view reflects modeled economics in the observed payment environment (Medicare-based payment proxy and state-level labor inputs). This lens is used as the primary business sustainability view, since outpatient clinic ownership/operations may not map directly to a therapist’s personal cost-of-living experience.
* RPP-adjusted lens (secondary for therapist value capture):
* A separate RPP-adjusted view deflates modeled revenue using rpp\_cost\_of\_living\_index and recomputes margin/ratio outputs. This lens is used as a secondary interpretation layer to better compare therapist compensation/value-capture patterns across states on a purchasing-power basis.
* Why both lenses are useful:
* The nominal lens is stronger for discussing modeled business sustainability under current payment conditions, while the RPP-adjusted lens is stronger for discussing therapist purchasing power and compensation share across states.
* Important modeling note:
* In the current implementation, labor\_cost\_per\_hour is already RPP-adjusted in the base model. The RPP-adjusted lens adds a purchasing-power adjustment to the revenue side and recomputes downstream metrics for consistency.
* Interpretation caution:
* Results should be interpreted as state-level modeled comparisons under explicit assumptions (UPH scenarios, Medicare-based revenue proxy, wage estimation method), not as direct measures of individual clinic profitability or therapist take-home pay.
* UPH scenarios were established for understanding that more than one unit can be utilized per hour of treatment time, and also to establish equal comparison level to wage numbers also being per hour. This assumption was one of my most challenging piece of ambiguous data for this project, due to federal regulations requiring 1:1 time for medicare payment. I did not want to estimate or extrapolate too much from contracted rates for commercial insurance, as these rates can vary widely across regions and therapy groups. The assumptions of 3.75, 4.25, and 4.75 were established to respect 1:1 regulations for medicare compliance, along with estimating payor mix of other insurance carriers. While this is my most questioned assumption, the use of views in this project, allow for this number to be manipulated as requested for the appropriate stakeholders
* Outpatient PT wage was not available to me from the public BLS dataset utilized. National all-industry PT, National outpatient PT and state all-industry PT wages were available to me. To create each state's individual outpatient wage estimation, I compared national outpatient PT wage to all-industry PT wage, and got a ratio of 0.922143. This number was then applied to each individual states state all-industry wage to provide a consistent and data-driven estimate across states. 25% benefits assumption was utilized for taking reported salaries and considering full cost of PT with benefits.
* Regional Price Parities (RPP) utilized to provide cost of living adjustment across states for col adjusted labor and revenue, and understanding of buying power available to physical therapists. This is to prevent appearing that physical therapists in high cost of living states, with assumption of also higher salaries means that they have better purchasing power. This is to provide a better standardized score between high cost of living and low cost of living states. Labor side is RPP-adjusted with revenue ide currently uses observed payment proxy at this time.
* State-level aggregation limits are the 2nd largest assumption/limitation in my data set that I acknowledge. While CMS does provide regional price adjustments, this project was utilized to analyze if those were truly enough when breaking down other factors involved included in the employment and business model.
